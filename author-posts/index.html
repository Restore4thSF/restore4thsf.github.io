Jewel v. NSA Case Management Hearing

Jewel v. NSA is one of several cases against unconstitutional surveillance in the courts. This one is FIVE years old – in fact, it was originally founded on the facts revealed by Mark Klein, who spoke at our rally on 1984 Day. For background and further documents, please visit the Electronic Frontier Foundation website. Today, we heard a very interesting ruling from the judge in the case, that fundamentally benefits the plaintiffs (all US citizens and all AT&T customers, each represented by a lead plaintiff). But, it’s procedurally and strategically complex, so it bears some explanation.

 

TL;DR: A whole lot of evidence that was declassified or otherwise made public can now be used to establish standing in the case.

  • Daniel Ellsberg: Former US military analyst, author, and activist. Mr. Ellsberg famously leaked the Pentagon Papers to The New York Times in 1971, revealing the systematic deception of four presidencies as well as prompting the 1st Amendment case, NYT v. United States.

Restore the Fourth SF Bay Area Leadership

The members of the leadership were chosen by developing a strategy and starting it on their own, or by being the only volunteer for the job. Usually that leader or strategy was confirmed by vote in an open strategy meeting. These meetings are intended to be located and scheduled so that the maximum number of people can attend, and is a great way to introduce dissent. If you feel as though you want to change leadership, put it on the agenda at an open strategy meeting and demand a vote. If you want to become a leader via a new role, create that role, let us know and we’ll put you on this list.

Bay Area Advisory Board: Michael, Alex, Sina, Zaki, and Ryan

Threat Levels

We should distinguish between different threat levels because they require different levels of encryption security. We also have to be aware that the threat level only increases if your content is “worthwhile enough” so a way to avoid needing further encryption protection would be to avoid being noticed as worthwhile. The key to that is to hide metadata.

The threat level concept eventually breaks down, however, because in order to communicate outside the secure group, you are immediately downgraded to “lowest common denominator” encryption techniques.
